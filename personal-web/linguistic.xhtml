Presentation

Doing a comprehensive linguistic study of every witness that integrates a manuscript tradition can be a rather laborious work. A possible alternative to limit the object of study is, to my mind, to examine the most important linguistic phenomenon, namely linguistic variation. By focusing on all the language variants found after collating every witness, we report on the distinctive linguistic features of every manuscript, thus making the study of a manuscript tradition much more practicable.

For a period such as the Middle Ages, which featured rather flexible writing codes, textual variation can be very informative. Taking a corpus of formal features as a reference and comparing it with other texts, for which there is a high degree of certainty about the conditions in which they were written, can actually provide useful information regarding their writing standards, that is, the scriptorium.

Linguistic variants usually occur as a result of the self-dictation which the copyist does after reading and memorizing the extract he intends to copy, hence reflecting features of his idiolect. The study of those features contributes to gain a better understanding of the witness production, since it provides evidence of the human resources who participated in this production.

Copy errors are another important source of information, providing us information related to the sources used by the copyists, distinctive features of the model (e.g. a common graphic substitution in the copy means that the two graphemes were very similar in the model), and linguistic data, among others.

Therefore, in order to compile all these data I propose a digital edition based on the collation of every reading of every witness, an edition which is applied to the Galician-Portuguese secular lyric.

Beginning probably around the middle of the thirteenth century, the Galician-Portuguese songs, known as cantigas, began to be compiled in collections known as cancioneiros (songbooks). Three such anthologies are known: the Cancioneiro da Ajuda (A), the Cancioneiro Colocci-Brancuti (or Cancioneiro da Biblioteca Nacional de Lisboa) (B), and the Cancioneiro da Vaticana (V). For this project, I have selected a corpus of songs attending to diachronic criteria. I chose texts that are transmited by both A and B (and/or V). All texts preserved in the minor witnesses N, T, M and P have been selected too. [Include a link to an explanation on the selection.

Mark-up

  • I retrieved the corresponding diplomatic transcriptions that Prof. Mariña Arbor Aldea (A and T) and Antonio Guiadanes (B and V) have gracioulsy handed over to me.
  • I convert these transcriptions from plain text to XML (regex). I give every poem an ID, so that its different versions share the same ID.
  • I use XQuery to retrieve the witnesses of the songs that have the same ID but not the same number of lines. I manually correct those instances inserting <gap> elements in the missing lines with an @reason attribute (being damage and economy the most common values).
  • I mark-up the songs according to the Parallel Segmentation Method. I use XSLT to match the lines of the poems that have the same ID. Each line is tagged as follows:
    <l n="12">
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">mais se lle ren de mia coita disser</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B">mays selhi re<ex>n</ex> demha coyta diss<ex>er</ex></rdg>
    </app>
</l>
  • Manually, I correct the disposition of the text, joining and dividing the elements so they conform words. I must correct very specific irregularities so the tokens of each reading are aligned.
  • I use XSLT again to match the tokens. The tokens that match are displayed as text nodes that are children of <l>. The tokens that are different are displayed as variants inside an <app> element
    <l n="12">
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">mais</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B">mays</rdg>
    </app>
    se
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">lle</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B">lhi</rdg>
    </app>
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">ren</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B">re<ex>n</ex></rdg>
    </app> 
    de
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">mia</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B">mha</rdg>
    </app>
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">coita</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B">coyta</rdg>
    </app>
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">disser</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B">diss<ex>er</ex></rdg>
    </app>
</l>
  • I have created a Feature Structure library where I define every textual variant I find. I have a list of individual features (<f>) that are later combine to create feature structures (<fs>).
    <fs xml:id="dat" feats="#datCon #pr #morph #ling" n="Dative contamination">
    ↳
        <f name="linguisticVariant" xml:id="ling"/>
        <f name="morphosyntactic" xml:id="morph"/>
        <f name="pronoum" xml:id="pr"/>
        <f name="dativeContamination" xml:id="datCon"/>
  • At least one <rdg> in each <app> is linked to the feature library through and @ana attribute. Some of the variants are automatically tagged using XSLT to look for regular patterns.
    <l n="4">
    de 
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">mia</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B" ana="#trend">mha</rdg>
    </app>
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A" ana="#irreg">ui<ex>&#771;</ex>jr</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B" ana="#denas">uijr</rdg>
    </app>
    todo 
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">mi</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B" ana="#irreg">m'</rdg>
    </app>
    auen 
</l>
  • If in the same token I have more than one variant, I use <seg> with an @ana attribute to specify which part of the token corresponds with each value.
    <l n="1">
    De quant' eu sempre 
    <app>
        <rdg wit="#A">deseiei</rdg>
        <rdg wit="#B" ana="#trend #misread">
            dese<seg ana="#misread">l</seg>e<seg ana="#trend">y</seg>
        </rdg>
    </app>
</l>

Graphic variants

In the medieval texts we work with, graphic variants are the most common type of textual variation between witnesses. Considering we are mostly interested in linguistic variation, we have simplified our encoding of graphic variants so that we only identify the following types:

  • Graphic variants with an irregular distribution: when we cannot predict which copyist/witness will present that particular variant (e.g. abbreviation vs. full form).
  • Regular variant: when there is a predictable distribution of the graphic choices (e.g. the use of <ll> in A vs. the use of <lh> in B and V).
  • Orthographic tendency: even if its use is not completely regular, we can observe a trend for certain usages (e.g. the use of <m> in B as a nasal consonant in the word-ending position).
  • Occasional variant: when we register few instances of a certain variant.
  • Archaic spelling: when we interpret that the use of certain graphs is a trace of a previous source because it is not consistent with the scribe practices.
  • Punctuation.

By only registering the graphemes that change from witness to witness, we think we depict an accurate description of the particularities of each manuscript. In this manner, we eliminate the “normal” usages, allowing us to concentrate better on the distinctive ones.

As an example, let us analyze the uses of <i>, <j> and <y> in the Pergaminho Sharrer. Due to its small extension, we can analyze each one of the cases and compare the results obtained by collation (that is, by comparing it with the other witnesses that transmit the same songs and tagging the variation between them) and those got by studying all their occurrences (i.e. without taking into account the readings from other witnesses).

Variants obtained by collation

After analyzing this graph, we may conclude that the alternation between <y> and <i> in diphthongs and the use of <j> are the most striking features of this manuscript. Our perception might change, though, if we study all the occurrences of those three letters.

Internal variation

We confirmed that the normal way to represent the approximant palatal is indeed the <y>. By contrast, the instances of <i> in the same position seem now occasional compared to the uses of that same grapheme to represent the pure vowel. However, that is the expected and obvious use, a fact that anybody can see just by reading a few lines of the witness. That is why, in our opinion, the “few” instances of <i> as a diphthong are more relevant that its other uses.

The use of <j> could be considered in this graph a mere occasional variant. Nevertheless, the closer attention that the other graph demanded show us that 92,9% of the total instances of <j> occur after <u> or <m>. As we know, in the Gothic script the minims hardly connect to each other, making it difficult to distinguish <i>, <u>, <m>, and <n>. In spite of its few occurrences, we are right to consider the use of <j> to avoid this confusion as an orthographic tendency in the Pergaminho Sharrer that could have been easily overseen if we were to study the letters with the highest percentages inside the same manuscript.
